The highlight of our contributions is the following:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We propose several benchmark functions on which widely-used NSGA-II, NSGA-III and SMS-EMOA are proven to outperform the simple EMOA, (G)SEMO. As far as we know, such results have not been rigorously proven before. 
\item We introduce a classification of all multi-objective fitness functions, denoted ($a$,$b$)-Pareto-sparse, on which functions with large $a$ and small $b$ are shown to be difficult for (G)SEMO.
\item We provide a general lower bounds on the expected number of fitness evaluations for (G)SEMO to solve any ($a$,$b$)-Pareto-sparse functions, and this lower bound is exponential in $a$. Thus, the proposed benchmark functions are those with $a=\Omega(n)$, namely \textsc{OneTrapZeroTrap}, \textsc{OneJumpZeroJump}, and a class of instances of the \textsc{MaxSat} problem.
\item Conversely, we show that \textsc{OneTrapZeroTrap} can be solved by NSGA-II, NSGA-III and SMS-EMOA efficienly in time $O(n\log{n})$ with a mild diversity mechanism of avoiding genotype duplications in the population. 
\item In addition to our theoretical results, experiments conducted on \textsc{OneTrapZeroTrap} and the class of instances of the bi-objective \textsc{MaxSat} problem
show that the success rate for (G)SEMO is zero, while it is $100\%$ for NSGA-II, NSGA-III, and SMS-EMOA.
\end{enumerate}

Our published conference paper only focused on \textsc{OneTrapZeroTrap}, thus only point (4) and partially (3). Points (1), (2), (5), and a large part of (3) are new contributions, and we hope these meet the requirements of the journal for an extension of a conference paper. 

We look forward to hearing back from you and the reviewers.

Yours sincerely
